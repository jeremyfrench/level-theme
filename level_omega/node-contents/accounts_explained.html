Explained.

All private limited and public companies must supply a signed set of annual accounts to Companies House every year.

Depending on the size of a business, there are three different obligations for filing: small, medium or large. What determines these are a combination of turnover, balance sheet total and number of employees.

Large business

Files full set of accounts

  • Profit and loss account
  • Balance sheet
  • Auditor's report
  • Directors' report
  • Notes to the accounts
  • Group accounts

At least two of the following conditions must be met:

  1. Annual turnover more than £22.8 million
  2. Balance sheet total more than £11.4 million
  3. Average number of employees more than 250

Medium business

Files abbreviated accounts

  • Abbreviated profit & loss
  • Balance sheet
  • Special auditor's report
  • Directors' report
  • Notes to the accounts

At least two of the following conditions must be met:

  1. Annual turnover £22.8 million or less
  2. Balance sheet total £11.4 million or less
  3. Average number of employees 250 or fewer

Small
business

Files abbreviated accounts

  • Abbreviated balance sheet
  • Special auditor's report

At least two of the following conditions must be met:

  1. Annual turnover £5.6 million or less
  2. Balance sheet total £2.8 million or less
  3. Average number of employees 50 or fewer

Terminology.

The Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet shows the financial position of a business at the year end. It shows two important things, where the funds have come from (the Liabilities) and how the money has been spent (the assets). It represents one point in time and it can be referred to as the "snap shot".

Profit and Loss Account

The Profit and Loss Account shows the Net Profit or Loss the business has made within an accounting period. It shows the total revenues the company has made and the total expenses incurred to generate that revenue. Unlike the balance sheet, it shows all the revenue and expenses over the accounting period and is not a "snap shot". A Net Profit is made if total expenditure is less than total revenues.

The Balance Sheet

Asset

An item of value owned by the business

Fixed Assets

Assets used within the business and not acquired for the purposes of resale. Examples include:

  • Land and buildings
  • Plant and machinery
  • Fixtures and fittings
  • Motor vehicles
Tangible Assets

These include equipment, cash, plant, property, anything that has long-term physical existence and is not for sale to customers. They are shown at Net Book Value (after "wear and tear" has been deducted).

Current Asset

Assets used within the business to help generate wealth for the company. Examples include:

  • Raw materials, partially finished goods and finished goods
  • Debtors, customers that owe money to the business
  • Prepayments, amounts paid in advance for goods and services
  • Cash at bank
Depreciation

The measure of wearing out of a fixed asset. All fixed assets are expected to wear out, become less efficient and to get "tired".

Current Liability

Payments owed to other parties within one year and include suppliers, tax payable, overdrafts to banks, short term loans and dividends.

Debtors

The amounts owed to the business by customers.

Bad Debt Provision
The money a customer owes the business (from a prior credit sale) and does not pay.
Cash at Bank and in hand

The money held in the bank and in cash. If there is an overdraft it will not appear in Current Assets but will be found in the Current Liabilities section of the balance sheet.

Creditors

The amount owed to suppliers for products and services that were bought on credit.

Net Current Assets (Liabilities)

Sometimes referred to as the Working Capital, it is the money used to finance the business on a day to day basis.

Total Assets less Current Liabilities (Net Assets)

The difference between the Total Assets and Total Liabilities.

Capital and Reserves

Capital is the value of cash and shares (the basic unit of ownership of the company). Reserves are the retained profit and any increases in the value of shares or assets (mainly property).

Call up Share Capital

The unpaid amount of the issued shares that the holders are now called to pay.

Profit
There are many types of profit:
  • Cash surplus, receipts minus payments
  • Taxable profit, profit adjusted for tax purposes
  • Accounting profit, income received minus expenditure
Shareholders Funds
These are the reserves owned by the shareholders and include, share capital, retained profit and other reserves.
Shares
The basic unit of ownership of the company and represents the amounts invested in a company by its owners. Owners of the company are called shareholders.

Profit and Loss Account

Turnover
The sales value of goods and services sold to customers for the accounting period.
Cost of Sales
Cost of Sales is calculated by taking the value of goods purchased during the year and in stock at the beginning of the year, less stoke sold during the year.
Gross Profit
Gross Profit is calculated by deducting the Cost of Sales from the Turnover and can be seen as a measure of success in the marketplace.
Administrative Expenses
Referred to as Expenditure and including examples such as:
  • advertising
  • rent and rates
  • wages and salaries
  • travelling expenses
  • light and heat
  • office expenses
  • miscellaneous expenses
  • bank interest
  • loan interest
  • depreciation

The breakdown is not as detailed as in the management accounts; one reason is that companies do not want to reveal too much commercially sensitive information.

Operating Profit (Net Profit)
This is the next "layer" of profit and is reached by taking Expenses away from Gross Profit. This is a good measure of the company’s trading performance.
Interest Receivable
This is the profit made from investments (often interest from savings at the bank).
Interest Payable
The interest payable on loans, overdrafts and leases.
Profit on Ordinary Activities Before tax
What is left after total costs are deducted from total revenue and before the deduction of taxes and dividends.
Tax on Profit on Ordinary Activities
It is the tax that is owed based on the profit the company makes.
Dividends
The part of the profit paid per share to the shareholders.
(Loss) / Profit Retained for the financial year
This is the best measure of the success of the company over that accounting period. These profits are often used for investing in the future operations of the business. As an indicator of comparative performance, however, it is less valuable than Return On Investment (ROI), which is the measure of what a shareholder gets back from an investment in the company.
